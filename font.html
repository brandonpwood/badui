Properties of depth-first search


Depth-first search yields valuable information about the structure of a graph. Perhaps the most basic property of depth-first search is that the predecessor subgraph G does indeed form a forest of trees, since the structure of the depthfirst trees exactly mirrors the structure of recursive calls of DFS-VISIT. That is, u D : if and only if DFS-VISIT.G; / was called during a search of u’s adjacency list. Additionally, vertex is a descendant of vertex u in the depth-first forest if and only if is discovered during the time in which u is gray.
Another important property of depth-first search is that discovery and finishing times have parenthesis structure. If we represent the discovery of vertex u with a leboxes parenthesis “.u” and represent its finishing by a right parenthesis “u/”, then the history of discoveries and finishings makes a well-formed expression in the sense that the parentheses are properly nested.
